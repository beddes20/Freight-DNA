Update the recurring-lane outreach email generation in Lane Work Queue to use the following house style.

Goal
Make recurring-lane emails sound like a freight broker reaching out about real freight:
- direct
- conversational
- freight-native
- short
- operational
- not corporate
- not stiff
- not overly polished

Formatting rules
- Always format lanes as: City, ST → City, ST
- Never show duplicated state abbreviations
- Never use lowercased raw lane strings in the final email
- Never use decimal load counts like “5.10 loads per week”
- Convert weekly load volume into human phrasing like:
  - usually 5–7 a week
  - around 2–3 a week
  - about 1–2 a week
  - around 8–10 a week
- Keep the email to 3–4 short sentences max

Banned phrases
Do not use:
- carrier bench
- we value our relationship
- ongoing coverage
- reaching out about a recurring lane we run consistently
- averaging X.XX loads per week
- would love to connect
- keep you in mind for future opportunities

Preferred phrasing
Prefer language like:
- checking to see if you’ve got capacity
- does that fit your network?
- looking to line up steady coverage
- looking for coverage on this lane
- regular freight
- steady volume
- if so, I’d be glad to talk through it
- if this week’s tight, no worries

Tone rules
- Sound like a broker, not an account manager
- Keep it concise and scannable
- Use plain English
- Slightly vary sentence structure across drafts
- Do not copy examples verbatim
- Mention prior relationship only if it is actually true
- End with a simple operational ask

Preferred example
“Hey [Carrier Name] — checking to see if you’ve got capacity for [formatted lane]. We usually have [human weekly volume phrase] on this lane and are looking to line up steady coverage. Does that fit your network? If so, I’d be glad to talk through it.”

Variation examples
1. “Hey [Carrier Name] — wanted to see if you have room for [formatted lane]. We usually have [human weekly volume phrase] on it and are looking for steady coverage. Does that fit your network?”

2. “Hey [Carrier Name] team — checking on capacity for [formatted lane]. We move [human weekly volume phrase] on this lane and would like to line up regular coverage. Let me know if it makes sense for you.”

3. “Hey [Carrier Name] — we’ve got regular freight on [formatted lane], usually [human weekly volume phrase]. Wanted to see if this lane fits your network and if you’d want to talk through it.”

4. “Hey [Carrier Name] team — looking for coverage on [formatted lane]. We usually have [human weekly volume phrase] moving there. If that fits your network, I’d be glad to talk through details.”

5. “Hey [Carrier Name] — wanted to check if [formatted lane] lines up with where your trucks run. We usually have [human weekly volume phrase] on it and are looking to tighten up regular coverage.”

Implementation requirements
- Apply this style to AI-generated recurring-lane drafts
- Update the fallback recurring-lane template to match this tone
- Reuse centralized helpers for:
  - formatted lane display
  - weekly load volume phrasing
- Do not break existing send/draft endpoints or outreach logging
- If prompt text and fallback template live in separate files, update both so they stay aligned

Acceptance criteria
- Recurring-lane emails no longer contain stiff/corporate phrasing
- Emails do not contain “carrier bench”
- Emails do not contain decimal weekly load counts
- Emails use cleaned lane formatting
- Emails sound direct, short, and freight-native
- Drafts have light variation and do not all read identically

When finished, return:
1. exact files changed

4. any assumptions made
