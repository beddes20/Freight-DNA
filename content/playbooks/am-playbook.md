# Value Truck — Account Manager Playbook

*The Operating System for Retaining, Growing, and Deepening Shipper Relationships*

---

## Section 1: Purpose

This playbook is the operating system for Value Truck Account Managers. It is not a suggestion list — it is the standard. Every section defines exactly what you do, when you do it, how you do it, and what great looks like.

Your job is not to "manage" accounts. Your job is to become so embedded in your customer's supply chain that replacing you would be painful, expensive, and unthinkable.

**The Value Truck AM Formula:**

> Speed + Solutions + Consistency + Relationship Depth = Irreplaceable

This playbook drives five outcomes:

1. **Consistency** — Every account gets the same world-class operating rhythm
2. **Accountability** — Clear metrics, clear expectations, no gray area
3. **Revenue Growth** — Proactive lane expansion, wallet share capture, referral generation
4. **Relationship Depth** — Move every contact from transactional to trusted partner
5. **Retention** — Customers stay because leaving would hurt them

---

## Section 2: Role Expectations — What an Account Manager Is (and Isn't)

The AM role is a hybrid: part relationship builder, part growth driver, part operations quarterback. You own the customer experience after the initial sale. You are the face of Value Truck.

### Your Core Responsibilities

- Retain every account. Zero preventable churn.
- Grow every account. More lanes, more loads, more revenue, more contacts.
- Respond faster than anyone. 4 minutes or less — WITH a rate and a solution. Not "let me check."
- Know your freight cold. Load count, revenue, margin, trends — daily. No surprises.
- Build relationships so deep that price becomes secondary.
- Coordinate flawlessly with LMs, LCs, and leadership internally.
- Maintain a CRM that tells the full story of every account, every contact, every opportunity.

### What You Are Not

- You are not a load coordinator. You own the relationship and growth — ops handles execution details.
- You are not reactive. If you're only responding to inbound requests, you're losing.
- You are not a rate machine. You sell trust, speed, and certainty — not the lowest number.

### The Three Levels of AM Performance

**Good (Meets Standard):**
- Responds within 4 minutes consistently
- CRM is updated daily with notes and opportunities
- Hits load count and margin targets
- Retains assigned accounts with no preventable losses
- Completes all daily/weekly cadence items on time

**Great (Exceeds Standard):**
- Grows accounts 15%+ QoQ in load count or revenue
- Has 50%+ of contacts at 2nd base or higher
- Proactively identifies and pitches new lanes before the customer asks
- Generates 1+ referral per quarter from existing relationships
- Leads QBRs that customers actually look forward to

**Elite (Top 1%):**
- Grows accounts 25%+ QoQ consistently
- 75%+ of contacts at 3rd base or homerun
- Gets first access to RFPs, projects, and new site freight before competitors
- Customers call YOU for market intel and advice — you're an extension of their team
- Produces warm introductions that turn into new accounts for Value Truck
- Price is rarely the conversation. Trust and execution are proven.

## Section 3: New Account Handoff — The First 48 Hours

The handoff is where accounts are won or lost before they even start. A sloppy transition tells the customer they're just another number. A tight handoff tells them Value Truck is different.

### Before the Handoff Call

- [ ] Get the full download from the sales rep: lanes, volumes, contacts, SOPs, service requirements, pain points
- [ ] Research the customer: website, genlogs, news, acquisitions, site locations, inbound/outbound patterns
- [ ] Identify the customer's current core carriers and what makes them "core" (ask: "What 3-4 qualities do your best carriers have?")
- [ ] Know their annual freight volume or weekly load count — ideally broken out by site
- [ ] Set up the account in CRM and TMS before the call happens

### The Onboarding Call (Non-Negotiable — Even If Loads Are Already Moving)

**Who's on it:** AM, Sales Rep, Ops Lead, and ideally a Value Truck leader

**Purpose:** Set the tone that this is a partnership, not a transaction.

**Agenda:**
1. Introductions — who handles what on both sides
2. Confirm lanes, volumes, and service expectations
3. Communication preferences — email, phone, Teams, portal? How often? What triggers an escalation?
4. Stake your claim: "We are extremely good at [urgent freight / hard-to-cover lanes / your specific strength]. Send us the tough ones."
5. Set the expectation: "You'll hear from me daily. If something goes wrong, you'll hear from me before you have to ask."
6. Ask: "What does a perfect carrier partner look like to you?"

### Within 24 Hours of the Call

- [ ] Send a follow-up email recapping the call, confirming contacts, and including the team distribution list (DL)
- [ ] Create the internal team DL for the account
- [ ] Document everything in CRM: contacts, SOPs, preferences, KPIs, communication cadence
- [ ] Begin building the customer org chart — who reports to whom, who controls what freight
- [ ] Send your first rate or solution on an open load — even if it's a loss leader. Get freight moving. Build trust and relevance immediately.

> **Elite Move:** In your follow-up email, include one insight from your research. Example: "I saw you recently opened a facility in Laredo — we run 40+ loads/week on TX border lanes. Happy to help when that ramps up."

## Section 4: The 30/60/90-Day Success Plan for New Accounts

Every new account follows this timeline. No exceptions. This is how you go from "new vendor" to "trusted partner."

### Days 1–30: Prove It

**Goal:** Execute flawlessly. Build trust. Get to 1st base with every contact.

**Actions:**
- [ ] Complete onboarding call and full CRM setup (Day 1-2)
- [ ] Move first load within 5 business days — even at breakeven or a small loss. Relevance > margin early.
- [ ] Call every contact daily. Not just email. Pick up the phone.
- [ ] Respond to every quote/request in under 4 minutes WITH a rate and solution.
- [ ] Send proactive updates on every load — don't wait for them to ask.
- [ ] Ask for the hardest freight: "What lane gives you the most headaches? Let us take a shot at it."
- [ ] Document freight patterns: spot vs. contract, peak days, recurring lanes, seasonal surges.
- [ ] Build initial org chart: who reports to whom, who controls what freight, who are the influencers.
- [ ] Use genlogs + customer website to identify other sites, inbound/outbound patterns, and hidden freight.

**What to Say:**
- "I know we're new to you. My goal in the first 30 days is to prove we belong in your top 3 carriers. Send us the tough stuff."
- "What's the one thing your current carriers consistently drop the ball on? That's where I want to start."
- "I'm going to over-communicate early. I'd rather you tell me to slow down than wonder where your freight is."

**Scorecard at Day 30:**
- [ ] First load moved?
- [ ] All contacts at 1st base minimum?
- [ ] CRM fully built out (contacts, SOPs, notes)?
- [ ] Customer org chart started?
- [ ] Response time under 4 min consistently?
- [ ] Proactive research completed (genlogs, sites)?

### Days 31–60: Expand

**Goal:** Move relationships to 2nd base. Identify growth lanes. Conduct first QBR.

**Actions:**
- [ ] Conduct first monthly business review (QBR) with the customer
- [ ] Present a lane performance scorecard: on-time %, cost vs. market, communication score
- [ ] Pitch 2-3 new lanes based on your research (genlogs, site map, inbound/outbound patterns)
- [ ] Ask: "Who else on your team handles freight that I should connect with?"
- [ ] Start transitioning spot freight to contracted freight where possible
- [ ] Learn what makes each contact look good to their boss — then help them achieve it
- [ ] Begin personalizing communication — memes, gifs, texts, Teams messages. Be human.
- [ ] Tell on yourself: Share a specific success story. "Last week we covered a load in [difficult lane] in 18 minutes flat."

**What to Say:**
- "I pulled together our first 30 days of data. Can I walk you through it? I have some ideas on where we can add more value."
- "I noticed you're running freight out of [City]. We have strong capacity there. Can I take a look at those lanes?"
- "Can you help me find out who handles the outbound for your [Site Name] facility?"

**Scorecard at Day 60:**
- [ ] First QBR completed?
- [ ] 2+ contacts at 2nd base?
- [ ] New lane pitched or won?
- [ ] Load count growing vs. first 30 days?
- [ ] Any spot-to-contract conversions?
- [ ] New contact identified or introduced?

### Days 61–90: Embed

**Goal:** Become a trusted carrier. Get to 3rd base. Lock in contracted freight. Plan or complete first in-person visit.

**Actions:**
- [ ] Schedule or complete an in-person visit (lunch, site tour, or face-to-face meeting)
- [ ] Propose taking over lanes where incumbents are failing (service issues or overpriced)
- [ ] Request a warm introduction to another load planner, site, or division
- [ ] Identify and propose a contractual lane commitment with volume targets
- [ ] Conduct second QBR with a growth roadmap: "Here's where I think we can help you next quarter."
- [ ] Ask for a referral: "Who else in your network would benefit from this kind of service?"
- [ ] Begin positioning for upcoming RFPs or project freight

**What to Say:**
- "I'd love to come see your operation in person. I always find new ways to help once I see the dock."
- "I know you've been having trouble with [lane/carrier]. Let me put together a proposal to take that over."
- "We've proven ourselves on spot. Let's talk about locking in 3-5 lanes on contract so you get guaranteed capacity."
- "You've been great to work with. Honestly, if you know anyone in your network who needs this level of service, I'd love an introduction."

**Scorecard at Day 90:**
- [ ] In-person visit completed or scheduled?
- [ ] 1+ contacts at 3rd base?
- [ ] Contracted lane(s) won?
- [ ] Revenue growth of 25%+ vs. first 30 days?
- [ ] Referral requested?
- [ ] Warm introduction to new contact or site?
- [ ] Positioned for upcoming RFP or project freight?

## Section 5: The Operating Cadence — Daily, Weekly, Monthly

Consistency is the cheat code. The AM who runs the same cadence every single day, week, and month will outperform the "talented" AM who wings it. Every time.

### Daily (Every Single Day, No Exceptions)

**Morning Block (First 30 Minutes of Your Day):**
- [ ] Check overnight load statuses — any issues? Get ahead of them before the customer wakes up.
- [ ] Review today's pickups and deliveries across all accounts.
- [ ] Check your numbers: load count, revenue, margin — YTD, MTD, WTD. KNOW YOUR FREIGHT.
- [ ] Identify your top 3 proactive actions for the day (new lane pitch, follow-up, research, referral ask).

**Throughout the Day:**
- [ ] Call every active contact. Not email — call. The "what else" question happens on the phone, not in a subject line.
- [ ] Respond to every inbound request in 4 minutes or less — WITH a rate and a solution. Not "let me check and get back to you."
- [ ] Send proactive load updates before customers ask. "Your load picked up on time at 0800. ETA tomorrow 1400. Tracking link below."
- [ ] Escalate issues immediately. Call first, then email to document. Never let a customer discover a problem on their own.
- [ ] Coordinate with LMs and LCs on coverage, capacity, and any in-transit issues.

**End of Day (Last 15 Minutes):**
- [ ] Update CRM: log every call, note, opportunity, and relationship movement.
- [ ] Check: Did I move the needle on at least one relationship today?
- [ ] Check: Did I make at least one proactive growth move (new lane pitch, referral ask, research action)?
- [ ] Prep for tomorrow: anything time-sensitive first thing?

> **The 4-Minute Rule:** When a customer sends a quote request, load tender, or question, your response window is 4 minutes. Not 4 minutes to say "I'll check." Four minutes to come back with a RATE and a SOLUTION. This is the single fastest way to differentiate Value Truck. Be first in their inbox. Every time. If you can't quote in 4 minutes, respond in 4 minutes with: "Working on it right now. You'll have options by [specific time]."

### Weekly (Every Friday or Monday)

- [ ] **Account Health Check:** Review every account's weekly load count, revenue, and margin vs. targets.
- [ ] **Relationship Audit:** Where is every contact on the baseball diamond? What's the plan to advance each one?
- [ ] **Growth Pipeline:** Update your opportunity pipeline — new lanes identified, pitched, won, or lost.
- [ ] **Weekly Summary to Leadership:** One-page summary per account covering:
  - Load count vs. target
  - Revenue and margin
  - Key wins and service issues
  - Growth opportunities in pipeline
  - Relationship status (any contacts at risk?)
- [ ] **Competitive Intel:** Note any competitor activity you've picked up on (lost lanes, rate undercutting, service failures).
- [ ] **Send Customer Touchpoints:** Weekly performance snapshot to key contacts (on-time %, loads moved, market insights).

### Monthly

- [ ] **QBR Prep & Delivery:** Conduct a structured business review with every active account (see QBR section below).
- [ ] **Wallet Share Analysis:** What % of this customer's total freight do we have? What's the total available? Set a target.
- [ ] **Deep Research:** Refresh genlogs, news, acquisitions, new site openings for each account.
- [ ] **Org Chart Update:** Add new contacts, update titles, identify untapped decision-makers.
- [ ] **Lane Expansion Plan:** Identify 2-3 new lanes to pitch per account based on research.
- [ ] **Referral Campaign:** Ask your 3rd-base and homerun contacts for one warm introduction this month.
- [ ] **Personal Development:** Review one loss, one win, and one growth miss. What did you learn?

## Section 6: The Relationship Growth System — The Baseball Diamond

This is the engine of Value Truck's account growth. Every contact in every account lives somewhere on the baseball diamond. Your job is to move them forward — relentlessly, intentionally, one base at a time.

Score every contact in your CRM. Review weekly. If a contact isn't advancing, you need a plan or a conversation with your manager.

### 1st Base: New & Transactional

**What It Looks Like:**
- Fresh introduction. Calls and emails still feel formal and a little uncomfortable.
- We provide options and solutions quickly from the first opportunity.
- The relationship is 100% transactional — we're one of many carriers quoting loads.
- Revenue is sporadic.

**How to Advance to 2nd Base:**
- [ ] Execute flawlessly on every load. Zero excuses. Service failures at this stage are fatal.
- [ ] Respond in under 4 minutes, every time. Prove you're faster than their other options.
- [ ] Get freight moving — even a loss leader. Trust is built by doing, not talking.
- [ ] Find and solve a pain point they didn't ask you to solve.
- [ ] Stake your claim: "We are extremely good at [specific thing]. Let us prove it."
- [ ] Tell on yourself: Share a specific success story. "We covered an emergency reefer in Des Moines in 22 minutes last Tuesday."

**Time Target:** Every contact should reach 2nd base within 30-45 days of first interaction.

### 2nd Base: Trust Is Building

**What It Looks Like:**
- We've built trust through consistent execution.
- Communication is more personal — memes, gifs, texts, Teams messages. It feels natural.
- You know their interests outside of work. You know what makes them look good to their boss.
- Freight is still mostly transactional, but there's opportunity to convert spot to contract.
- They may introduce you to other team members who need help.
- Revenue is steadier.

**How to Advance to 3rd Base:**
- [ ] Schedule an in-person visit or lunch. Face time changes everything in freight.
- [ ] Ask directly: "What lane is giving you the most trouble right now? Let me take it over."
- [ ] Propose converting 2-3 spot lanes to contract with a volume commitment.
- [ ] Start texting/calling from your personal cell. Make it easy for them to reach you.
- [ ] Ask for a warm introduction: "Who else on your team should I be connected with?"
- [ ] Deliver a QBR that isn't just data — bring insights, market intel, and a growth proposal.
- [ ] Bail them out of a jam. The relationship-defining moments happen when things go wrong.

**Time Target:** 2nd to 3rd base should take 30-60 additional days of consistent execution.

### 3rd Base: Trusted Carrier

**What It Looks Like:**
- You're a trusted carrier. You've bailed them out more than once and they remember.
- You're texting and calling from your cell phone. You've met in person (or have firm plans to).
- You have the opportunity to take over lanes where incumbents are failing — on service or price.
- You're starting to become an extension of their supply chain.
- You get transparency into projects, chunks of freight, and freight that never hits an RFP.
- Freight is NOT just transactional. It's turning contractual with chunks of volume.
- Price starts to become secondary. They value reliability and the relationship.
- They give you warm introductions to other planners, even ones without huge needs.
- Revenue is chunky and growing.

**How to Advance to Homerun:**
- [ ] Become their market advisor. Share rate trends, capacity shifts, and seasonal intel proactively.
- [ ] Ask for a seat at the table: "When your next RFP comes up, I'd love to be in the conversation early."
- [ ] Propose a strategic partnership: dedicated capacity, volume discounts, or managed lane programs.
- [ ] Get introduced to leadership on their side. Move beyond load planners to directors and VPs.
- [ ] Deliver so consistently that they start defending you in their internal meetings.
- [ ] Start attending their industry events or inviting them to yours.

### Homerun: True Partnership

**What It Looks Like:**
- This person has become a genuine friend.
- You are absolutely an extension of their supply chain. They call you for market updates, eyes and ears, advice.
- You get first access to projects, newly acquired lanes, site openings, and surges.
- You get feedback on RFPs that only a handful of carriers receive.
- You're talked about in a positive light in their team meetings. Hot introductions flow naturally.
- Price is secondary. We are more than just a rate. Trust is proven and earned.
- You're dealing with projects, surges, and chunks of freight.
- Revenue is consistent and substantial.

**How to Protect a Homerun Relationship:**
- [ ] Never take it for granted. Homerun relationships need continuous investment.
- [ ] Even a week of PTO can cool a 3rd-base relationship. Have a bulletproof handoff plan.
- [ ] Continue delivering insights and market intel — don't go on autopilot.
- [ ] Find ways to add value beyond freight: introductions, industry knowledge, problem-solving.
- [ ] Ask for referrals consistently. Homerun contacts are your best source of new business.
- [ ] Celebrate wins together. Send a note when they get promoted or their company hits a milestone.

## Section 7: Customer Growth Strategy — How to Expand Every Account

Growth is not optional. Every account has more freight than they're giving you. Your job is to find it, ask for it, and earn it.

### The 5 Growth Levers

**1. Lane Expansion**
- Use genlogs, customer websites, and site maps to find lanes they haven't offered you yet.
- Ask directly: "Can you help me find out who handles the outbound for your [City] site?"
- Research inbound and outbound at every facility. Many AMs only look at one direction.
- Look at similar customers in the same industry. If Customer A ships out of Memphis, Customer B probably does too.

**2. Wallet Share Capture**
- Know the total: How many loads/week does this customer ship across ALL carriers?
- Know your share: What % of that total do we handle?
- Set a target: "We have 8% of their freight. Goal is 20% by end of Q3."
- Identify where competitors are underperforming — that's your opportunity.

**3. Spot-to-Contract Conversion**
- Every recurring spot lane is a contract opportunity.
- After running 10+ loads on a lane, propose: "We've been consistent on this lane. Let's lock it in with a rate agreement."
- Contract freight = predictable revenue + deeper relationship + harder for competitors to displace.

**4. Competitive Displacement**
- Never badmouth competitors. Let your service speak.
- Listen for signals: late trucks, claims, poor communication, rate spikes.
- When you hear frustration: "I'd love to take a shot at that lane. No commitment — just let me show you what we can do."
- Propose a trial: 5-10 loads, specific KPIs, head-to-head comparison.

**5. Referral Generation**
- You earn the right to ask for referrals at 3rd base and homerun.
- Script: "You've been great to work with. If you know anyone in your network who could use this level of service, I'd love a warm introduction."
- Ask after a specific win: "We crushed it on that surge last week. Know anyone else who deals with that kind of volume?"
- Track every referral in CRM. Close the loop with the person who referred you.

### The QBR (Quarterly Business Review)

Monthly for the first 90 days, then quarterly. This is your #1 strategic selling tool.

**QBR Agenda:**
1. Performance Scorecard: on-time %, loads moved, cost vs. market rate, service incidents
2. Value Delivered: cost savings, emergency coverage, capacity guarantees
3. Market Intelligence: rate trends in their key lanes, capacity outlook, seasonal forecasts
4. Growth Roadmap: new lanes proposed, wallet share targets, upcoming opportunities
5. The Ask: specific freight you want, contacts you want to meet, RFP visibility you want

**Who Should Attend:**
- Your side: AM, Ops Lead, and (for strategic accounts) a Value Truck leader
- Their side: Your primary contact + their manager (push for this)

> **Elite QBR Move:** End every QBR with a "Next 90 Days" slide showing exactly what you plan to deliver. This turns a review into a commitment. It gives the customer something to hold you to, and it differentiates you from every other broker who shows up with a pie chart and leaves.

## Section 8: Escalation & Service Recovery — When Things Go Wrong

Service failures happen in freight. The difference between good and great is not avoiding problems — it's how fast and how well you recover. Present solutions, not excuses.

- **Rule #1:** The customer should NEVER discover a problem before you tell them.
- **Rule #2:** Call first. Then email to document. Never lead with email on a service failure.
- **Rule #3:** Come with the problem AND the solution. Never call just to deliver bad news.

### Escalation Tiers

**Tier 1 — AM Handles (Response: Immediate)**
- Late pickup or delivery (under 2 hours)
- Tracking/update requests
- Minor rate disputes or accessorial questions
- Driver communication issues
- Action: Resolve, communicate to customer within 15 minutes, log in CRM.

**Tier 2 — AM + Ops Leadership (Response: Within 30 Minutes)**
- Service failure on a high-value or high-visibility load
- Repeated service issues on the same lane or account
- Freight claims under $5,000
- Carrier no-shows requiring backup truck
- Action: Loop in ops leadership, provide customer with a written recovery plan within 2 hours.

**Tier 3 — Executive Escalation (Response: Within 1 Hour)**
- Account at risk of churning
- Claims over $5,000
- Customer leadership has escalated internally
- Systemic pattern of failures (3+ issues in 30 days)
- Action: Value Truck leadership calls the customer directly. Written root cause + corrective action plan within 24 hours.

### The Service Recovery Call Script

1. **Own It:** "I want to be upfront with you — we had [specific issue] on load [number]."
2. **Explain (briefly):** "Here's what happened: [1-2 sentences, no excuses]."
3. **Solve It:** "Here's what we're doing right now: [specific corrective action]."
4. **Prevent It:** "Here's what we're changing so this doesn't happen again: [systemic fix]."
5. **Rebuild:** "I know we need to earn back your confidence on this. I'd like to [specific next step]."

> **Elite Move:** After a Tier 2 or Tier 3 recovery, send a written summary to the customer within 24 hours: "What happened. What we did. What we changed. What you can expect going forward." This document becomes evidence that you handle problems better than their other carriers.

### Document Everything

- Every escalation logged in CRM with: date, issue, root cause, resolution, time to resolve.
- Review escalation trends monthly. Patterns = systemic problems that need a process fix.
- Bring escalation data to your QBRs — show the customer you track and improve.

## Section 9: CRM Standards — If It's Not in the CRM, It Didn't Happen

Your CRM is not a chore — it's your memory, your sales tool, and your insurance policy. If you leave and someone takes over your accounts, they should be able to pick up exactly where you left off.

### Daily CRM Requirements (Non-Negotiable)

- [ ] Log every customer call (even 2-minute check-ins) with: who, what was discussed, next action
- [ ] Update any contact's relationship stage (baseball diamond) if it changed
- [ ] Log any new opportunity: new lane, new contact, RFP intel, referral lead
- [ ] Document any service issue and resolution

### What Every Account Record Must Contain

- Company overview: industry, size, annual freight spend (estimated if needed)
- All contacts with: name, title, role, phone, email, baseball diamond stage, personal notes
- Org chart: who reports to whom, who controls what freight
- Freight profile: lanes, modes, volumes (spot vs. contract), peak seasons, special requirements
- Communication preferences: email, phone, Teams, portal? Frequency? Escalation triggers?
- Customer KPIs: what they measure us on (on-time %, communication, cost, flexibility)
- SOPs: pickup/delivery requirements, accessorials, appointment scheduling, BOL handling
- Growth pipeline: lanes being pursued, wallet share target, upcoming RFPs
- Competitive landscape: who else hauls for them, where competitors are strong/weak
- Escalation history: what went wrong, how we fixed it, what changed

### CRM Quality Standard

The **"Bus Test"**: If you got hit by a bus tomorrow, could another AM pick up your accounts on Monday and not miss a beat? If the answer is no, your CRM isn't done.

## Section 10: Internal Communication & Collaboration

You can't serve the customer well if internal communication is sloppy. The AM is the quarterback — you connect the customer to the right people inside Value Truck and make sure nothing falls through the cracks.

### Daily Internal Coordination

- [ ] Morning sync with your Load Managers (LMs) and Load Coordinators (LCs):
  - What's on the board today? Any coverage concerns? Any customer-specific requirements?
- [ ] Flag any high-priority or high-visibility loads that need extra attention.
- [ ] Share relevant customer intel with ops: "This customer is testing us on this lane. Execute perfectly."

### Weekly Leadership Sync

- [ ] Submit weekly account summaries (see Section 5 for format)
- [ ] Flag growth opportunities that need leadership support (executive introductions, pricing strategy)
- [ ] Report any at-risk accounts and your recovery plan
- [ ] Collaborate on strategic growth plays — don't go at it alone

### PTO Handoff Protocol (Non-Negotiable)

Before any time off, complete this checklist:

- [ ] Written handoff document covering every active account: key contacts, in-transit loads, pending issues
- [ ] Introduce the covering AM to your key contacts via email or call BEFORE you leave
- [ ] Update CRM so the covering AM can find everything they need
- [ ] Brief the covering AM verbally: "Here's what could go sideways while I'm out."
- [ ] Set clear expectations with customers: "I'll be out [dates]. [Name] is covering. They're fully briefed."

> **Warning:** Even 2nd and 3rd base relationships cool off after a week of PTO. When you return: Call every key contact Day 1 back. "I'm back. What did I miss? What do you need?"

## Section 12: Leadership Coaching Cadence

Great AMs aren't just born — they're coached. This section is for AM leadership to drive consistent development.

### Weekly 1:1 (Manager + AM, 30 Minutes)

**Agenda:**
1. **Numbers:** Load count, revenue, margin vs. targets. Where are you? Where should you be?
2. **Accounts:** Walk through your top 3 accounts. What's the plan this week for growth?
3. **Relationships:** Which contacts advanced on the diamond? Which are stuck? Why?
4. **Pipeline:** What new opportunities are you pursuing? What's stalled?
5. **Blockers:** What do you need from leadership to move faster?
6. **One Win + One Miss:** What went well this week? What did you learn from a miss?

### Monthly Account Reviews (Manager + AM Team, 60 Minutes)

- Each AM presents their top growth account and their biggest challenge account.
- Team brainstorms solutions and shares what's working in their own accounts.
- Review team-level KPIs: total load count, revenue, retention, relationship distribution.
- Recognize top performers. Share specific examples of elite behavior.

### Quarterly Skill Development

- **Call listening/review:** Listen to 2-3 customer calls per AM. Coach on language, tone, and strategy.
- **QBR dry runs:** Practice QBR presentations before delivering to customers.
- **Role play:** Practice service recovery calls, referral asks, competitive displacement pitches.
- **Market knowledge:** Ensure AMs understand macro freight trends affecting their customers.

### Coaching Principles for Managers

- Coach to the playbook, not to your personal style. The system works when everyone runs it.
